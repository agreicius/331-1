Corollary 2.28.

Let \(R\) be a nontrivial commutative ring. If \(R\) is an integral domain, then \(R[x]\) is an integral domain, and conversely.

Proof.

Assume \(R\) is an integral domain. If \(f\) and \(g\) are nonzero polynomials in \(R[x]\text{,}\) then \(\deg f\in \Z_{\geq 0}\) and \(\deg g\in \Z_{\geq 0}\text{,}\) in which case
\begin{gather*} \deg fg=\deg f+\deg g \in \Z_{\geq 0}\text{.} \end{gather*}
In particular, \(\deg fg\ne -\infty\text{,}\) and thus \(fg\ne 0\text{.}\) This proves \(R[x]\) is an integral domain.

The reverse implication is trivial since \(R\) is a subring of \(R[x]\text{.}\)
