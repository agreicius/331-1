Example 2.72. Prime ideals of \(\Z\).

Let \(n\) be a nonzero integer. Prove that ideal \((n)\) is prime in \(\Z\) if and only if \(n=p\) is a prime integer.

Solution.
Since by definition prime ideals are not equal to the ring itself, and since by definition prime integers are greater than \(1\text{,}\) we may assume that \(n> 1\text{.}\) In this case, we have
\begin{align*} (n) \text{ prime} \amp \iff a,b\in (n) \text{ implies } a\in (n) \text{ or } (b)\in (n) \\ \amp \iff n\mid ab \text{ implies } n\mid a \text{ or } n\mid b\text{.} \end{align*}
The last line is in fact equivalent to \(n\) being a prime integer, though this is not immediate from the definition we gave originally involving factors. We give that argument below, but observe that we will prove something more general (using similar arguments) when discussing unique factorization domains.

Letâ€™s see why our original definition of a prime integer is equivalent to the last line in our chain of equivalences above. Assume \(n\) satisfies
\begin{align*} n\mid ab \amp \implies n\mid a\text{ or } n\mid b \end{align*}
for all \(a,b\in \Z\text{.}\) Now suppose \(q\mid n\) for some positive integer \(q\text{.}\) In that case, we have \(n=qr\) for some positive integer \(r\text{.}\) Since \(n\mid n=qr\) we must have \(n\mid q\) or \(n\mid r\text{.}\) Since \(q\mid n\) and \(r\mid n\) and \(q,r^gt; 0\text{,}\) it follows that in the first case \(q=n\) and \(r=1\text{,}\) and in the second case \(r=n\) and \(q=1\text{.}\) This shows that the only positive factors of \(n\) are \(1\) and \(n\text{.}\) Since \(n> 1\text{,}\) we conclude that \(n=p\) is a prime integer. The other direction is easier, making use of unique factorization.
